What is Big O Notation?

Big O is a notation for measuring the performance of an algorithm. Big O notation mathematically describes the complexity of an algorithm in terms of time and space. We don‚Äôt measure the speed of an algorithm in seconds (or minutes!). We measure the rate of growth of an algorithm in the number of operations it takes to complete.

The O is short for ‚ÄúOrder of magnitude‚Äù. So, if we‚Äôre discussing an algorithm with O(n), we say its order of magnitude, or rate of growth, is n, or linear complexity.

You will probably read or hear Big O referred to as asymptotic runtime, or asymptotic computational complexity. This is a fancy way of describing the limits of a function. There is a branch of mathematics, order theory, devoted to this topic. For our intents and purposes, order:

How Does Big O Notation Work?

Big O notation measures the worst-case runtime.

Why?

Because we don‚Äôt know what we don‚Äôt know.

If we‚Äôre writing a search algorithm, we won‚Äôt always know the query ahead of time. If we‚Äôre writing a sorting algorithm, we won‚Äôt always know the dataset ahead of time. What if the query is the very last element or what if the dataset is a real mess. We want to know just how poorly our algorithm will perform.

The worst-case scenario is also known as the ‚Äúupper bound‚Äù. Limits again!

Before we get into any code, let‚Äôs get hands-on to get a feel (pun intended) for Big O. We‚Äôll use an example from Grokking Algorithms.

Let‚Äôs say I give you a square piece of paper and ask you to divide it into sixteen squares. How would you approach this problem?

You could take the brute force approach and draw sixteen individual squares. If you take this approach, how many steps, or computations, will you perform?

Sixteen.

Is there an approach that requires fewer steps? Of course!

Fold the paper in half. Then in half again. Four squares!

Now fold it in half two more times.

When you unfold it, the paper will be divided into sixteen squares.

How many steps, or computations, were required?

Four.

In Big O notation, our first approach, brute force, is O(n), or linear time. Creating sixteen squares requires sixteen operations. But our second, refactored and optimized, approach is O(log n), or logarithmic time (the inverse of exponentiation). Creating sixteen squares requires only four steps.

We‚Äôll look at O(log n) later. Let‚Äôs begin with O(1), which will help us understand O(n).

What is our best-case scenario for this algorithm?

What if state is not equal to danger? What if state does not match any of our conditions? What is our worst-case scenario?

It‚Äôs still O(1).

Why?

Even though we check multiple conditions before returning cornflowerblue, the order of magnitude, or rate of growth, is constant. The size of the input does not affect the number of operations performed. We know the upper bound, or worst-case scenario, in advance, and we know it will not change.
